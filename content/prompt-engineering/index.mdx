# Overview

Prompt engineering is the practice of designing the instructions, context, and examples you give a language model so that it reliably produces the output you want. It sits between "the model" and "the product" — the same underlying model can feel unreliable or exceptional depending entirely on how it's prompted.

This guide covers prompt engineering from first principles through to the techniques used in production LLM applications, and — unlike most guides on the topic — treats **evaluation** as a first-class part of the discipline. Writing a good prompt is easy. Knowing that it's still good after your tenth revision, or after the underlying model changes, is the hard part. That's where [Langfuse](/) comes in: everything in this guide is written with an eye toward how you'd actually test, version, and monitor a prompt once it's live.

## Who this is for [#who-this-is-for]

- Developers building an LLM feature for the first time who want a mental model beyond "just ask nicely."
- Teams with a prompt that mostly works who want to make it more reliable, or need to prove it still works after a change.
- Anyone comparing prompting techniques (chain-of-thought, few-shot, ReAct, and so on) and wondering which one actually applies to their problem.

## The core loop [#the-core-loop]

Prompt engineering in production isn't a one-time writing exercise — it's a loop:

1. **Write** a prompt based on the task and a few examples.
2. **Trace** real requests to see what the model actually does with it, not just what you expected.
3. **Evaluate** outputs against a dataset or a judge to get a number, not a vibe.
4. **Version** the prompt so you can compare "before" and "after" and roll back if a change regresses quality.
5. Repeat, ideally as part of your normal development and CI process rather than an occasional audit.

Steps 1–2 are what most prompting guides focus on. Steps 3–5 are what actually determines whether a prompt survives contact with real users and a changing model landscape — and they're where Langfuse's [prompt management](/docs/prompt-management/overview) and [evaluation](/docs/evaluation/overview) tooling fits in.
