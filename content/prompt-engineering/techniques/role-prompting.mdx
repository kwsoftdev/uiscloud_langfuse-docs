# Role prompting

Role prompting asks a model to respond as if it were a particular persona — "You are a senior security engineer reviewing this code" — rather than giving instructions with no framing. It's one of the oldest and most overused prompting tricks, and it's worth understanding what it genuinely changes versus what's folklore.

## What it reliably changes [#what-it-changes]

- **Vocabulary and register.** "You are a patient elementary school teacher" versus "You are a terse senior engineer" produces noticeably different word choice, sentence length, and level of explanation — this is the most consistent effect of role prompting.
- **Scope of consideration.** "You are a security auditor reviewing this pull request" nudges the model toward looking for a specific category of issue (vulnerabilities) rather than general code quality, simply by narrowing what the model treats as relevant.
- **Format conventions.** A role tied to a real profession often comes with implicit format expectations (a "legal reviewer" role tends to produce more hedged, qualification-heavy language) that can save you from spelling out formatting rules explicitly.

## What it doesn't reliably change [#what-it-doesnt-change]

- **Underlying capability.** Telling a model "you are a world-class mathematician" does not make it better at arithmetic it would otherwise get wrong — a role is framing, not a capability upgrade, and testing this claim on your own tasks will reliably disappoint you if you're counting on it for accuracy gains.
- **Guaranteed accuracy in the claimed domain.** A role can shift tone and framing without improving factual correctness — treat any accuracy improvement from a role prompt as something to verify, not assume.

## A more useful framing: describe the job, not the character [#describe-the-job]

Instead of reaching for a persona as a shortcut, it's often more effective to be explicit about what you actually want the persona to imply:

```text
# Less useful — vague persona, unclear what it's meant to change
You are an expert customer support agent.

# More useful — states the actual constraints the persona was standing in for
Respond in a warm, concise tone. Never apologize more than once per response.
Offer a concrete next step in every reply. Do not use exclamation points.
```

The second version is longer, but it's also testable and debuggable in a way "be an expert" isn't — if the output doesn't match, you know exactly which instruction to revise.

## Where role prompting is genuinely useful [#where-its-useful]

- **Multi-agent or multi-perspective setups**, where different roles are deliberately given different information or objectives (a "proposer" and a "critic" reviewing the same output) — here the role is doing real structural work, not just flavor text.
- **Shorthand for an audience**, when you want output written _for_ a particular reader ("explain this as if the reader is a first-year CS student") rather than _as_ a particular character.
- **Consistency across a long system prompt**, where restating "as a support agent, you should..." throughout keeps the model anchored to a consistent frame rather than drifting mid-conversation.
